Then, we simply want to reduce the mass (3), volume (4) and price (5) of the accumulator system as much as possible to improve vehicle dynamics and value.

\subsection{Supercapacitor Detailed Analysis}

I first want to explore supercapacitors, and then compare them to Li-Po batteries later as a benchmark for top performance.

Supercapacitors store energy as charge electrostatically, allowing for very high power density and rapid charge/discharge cycles. The highest energy density supercapacitors are EDLCs (Electric Double Layer Capacitors). (TODO: either add source or table with different supercap chemistries and their properties)

\begin{equation}
E=\frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{1}{2} C (V_{max}^2-V_{min}^2)
\label{eq:capacitor_energy}
\end{equation}

Where E is energy in joules, C is capacitance in farads, and V is voltage in volts.

From Equation \eqref{eq:capacitor_energy}, it is clear that voltage sag has a large impact on usable energy capacity, and that we must consider voltage ranges. The upper limit is set by the maximum voltage allowed in FS-EV rules (600 V), while the lower limit is set by the inverter and motor minimum desirable voltage. With the motor selected by David, we can assume a minimum voltage of 400V (TODO: check with David and insert motor name and min voltage).

\begin{equation}
C=\frac{2 E}{V_{max}^2-V_{min}^2}=\frac{2 \cdot 320\text{ kJ}}{600\text{ V}^2-400\text{ V}^2}=3.2\text{ F}
\label{eq:required_capacitance}
\end{equation}

To achieve the required capacitance of 3.2 F at 600 V, we must connect multiple cells together. For equally rated cells, the total capacitance for series connections is given by:

\begin{equation}
C_{series}=\frac{C_{i}}{n}
\label{eq:series_capacitance}
\end{equation}

Where \(C_i\) is the individual cell capacitance, and n is the number of cells in series or parallel.

So for our design, assuming cell voltage of 3 V, we need 200 cells in series to reach 600 V. Then, to achieve the required capacitance of 3.2 F, we need 640F cell capacitance.

Another key paramter is the Equivalent Series Resistance (ESR), which for many supercapacitors in series becomes an important cell consideration. The total voltage sag under load can be calculated as:

\begin{equation}
V_{sag}=I_{peak} \cdot ESR_{total}=\frac{P}{V_{min}} \cdot ESR_{cell} \cdot n
\label{eq:voltage_sag}
\end{equation}

Where \(I_{peak}\) is the peak current drawn from the pack, and \(ESR_{total}\) is the total ESR of the pack equal to the cell ESR multiplied by the number of cells, n.

\subsection{Technology Selection Decision}

In this section, I want to compare the 3 different technologies (EDLC, Li-Po and LIC) based on their mass, volume, price, performance and availability.

Li-io Capacitors (LIC) have major availability issues, and data is not publically avaialable on cells built for such an application. Since this is a design project, and I cannot source reliable test data. Furthermore, their voltage mass cell density is actually worse than EDLC supercapacitors, even if their energgy is considerably better. To achieve 600V, one must use a DC-DC converter, but the added weight would lead to the overall mass becoming similar as before, whilst also drastically increasing complexity. For these reasons, I have decided to not consider LICs further in this report.

High-power Li-Po batteries are already utilised widely within Formula Student for top perfoming teams. With this in mind, I decide that the best approach would be to use best-in class Li-Po cells as a benchmark for supercapacitor performance.

First, I want to consider the easily quantifiable metrics of mass, volume and price. I employed a weighted scoring system using the following equation:

\textbf{Topology:} Implements a scalable ``Master-Slave'' architecture using NXP MC33771 battery cell controllers, supporting modular expansion across all 5 accumulator segments.
\textbf{Communication Interface:} Utilizes NXP's TPL (Twisted Pair Link) differential protocol. This current-mode transmission method provides high immunity to electromagnetic interference (EMI) generated by the tractive system inverter.
\textbf{Galvanic Isolation:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Daisy-chain communication links are bridged by \textbf{470pF High-Voltage Safety Capacitors} ($C4, C5$).
    \item These capacitors maintain the isolation barrier between stacked voltage domains (e.g., separating Segment 1 at 0--60V from Segment 2 at 60--120V) while blocking DC high voltage.
    \end{itemize}
\textbf{Layout Strategy:} Signal traces for \texttt{COM\_IN} and \texttt{COM\_OUT} are routed as differential pairs with $100\,\Omega$ impedance matching to preserve signal integrity.


\subsubsection{Cell Voltage Monitoring}
\textbf{Anti-Aliasing Filter Design:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Components: $R = 100\,\Omega$, $C = 47\,\text{nF}$.
    \item The cutoff frequency $f_c$ is calculated to reject high-frequency switching noise from the inverter (typically 8--16 kHz harmonics):
        \[
        f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi R C} = \frac{1}{2\pi (100)(47 \times 10^{-9})} \approx 33.8\,\text{kHz}
        \]
    \end{itemize}
\textbf{Passive Balancing Calculations:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Mechanism: Dissipative balancing is achieved via switchable resistors ($R_{bal} = 33\,\Omega$).
    \item \textbf{Balancing Current:} At a nominal maximum cell voltage of 4.2V:
        \[
        I_{bal} = \frac{V_{cell}}{R_{bal}} = \frac{4.2\,\text{V}}{33\,\Omega} \approx 127\,\text{mA}
        \]
    \item \textbf{Power Dissipation:} To determine the required component package size:
        \[
        P_{diss} = I_{bal}^2 \times R_{bal} = (0.127)^2 \times 33 \approx 0.53\,\text{W}
        \]
    \item \textbf{Constraint:} This dissipation necessitates the use of \textbf{1210 or 2010 package resistors} (rated for $>0.75$W) to ensure thermal stability during balancing cycles.
    \end{itemize}

\subsection{Segment Design}

The first step is to design a replicable segment design. Each segment will contain 40 cells connected in series, with cell holders to ensure secure mounting and electrical isolation.

Due to welding considerations, each cell must be separated by a busbar (and not simply like a typical AA battery connected in series), to prevent mechanical stress on the cell with thermal expansion. This leads to a more complex wiring harness, but is necessary for safety and reliability.

Packing density is maximised by arranging cells in a honeycomb pattern compared to a simpler rectangular pattern. However, further analysis showed that due to the uneven endings of the honeycomb arrangement, the overall volume was only reduced by 1\% (TODO: insert reference to work here), so a rectangular arrangement was selected for simplicity of manufacturing.

Each segment is best split into 2 modules of 20 cells each, both 5*4 cells in a rectangular arrangement. With this arrangement, the segment becomes an easily packaged cubiod shape.
